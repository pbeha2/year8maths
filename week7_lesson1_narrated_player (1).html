Hook
The problem with unknowns
When numbers change, we need a different tool
Jordan tries to calculate earnings the normal way:
Earnings = $3 × ??? + $150 × ???
The question marks are the problem. We can't calculate with unknowns — unless we give them names.

In algebra, we replace unknowns with letters. Not because mathematicians like confusing people — because it lets us write rules that work for any value, not just one.
Concept
Pronumeral
A letter standing in for an unknown number
Pronumeral
A letter used to represent a number that is unknown or that changes. It is the variable — the part of the expression that can take different values.
v = view-thousands  ·  s = sponsorships
v represents view-thousands — Jordan earns $3 per thousand views, but we don't know how many thousands yet, so we use v.

s represents the number of sponsorships — unknown each month, so we use s.

The letter chosen is not random — pick something that makes sense for the situation.
Concept
Coefficient
The number multiplied by the pronumeral
Coefficient
The number written directly in front of a pronumeral. It tells you the rate — how much you earn, lose, or gain per unit of the pronumeral.
In 3v, the coefficient is 3
3 in 3v means Jordan earns $3 for every view-thousand. The coefficient is always the multiplier.

150 in 150s means $150 per sponsorship. If Jordan had a better deal — say $200 per sponsorship — the expression would change to 200s.

The coefficient changes when the rate changes. The pronumeral stays the same.
Concept
Constant term
The number that never changes
Constant term
A standalone number in an expression — it is not multiplied by a pronumeral and its value never changes, regardless of what the pronumerals equal.
50 — Jordan's fixed monthly platform bonus
Jordan's platform pays a guaranteed $50 per month just for being an active creator. It doesn't matter how many views or sponsorships Jordan gets — that $50 is always there.

That's a constant. It has no pronumeral attached to it. It stands alone in the expression.
Concept
Term
One piece of the expression
Term
A single part of an algebraic expression — separated from other terms by + or − signs. A term can be a number, a pronumeral, or a number multiplied by a pronumeral.
3v  ·  150s  ·  50 — three separate terms
Count the terms by counting the + or − signs and adding one.

3v + 150s + 50 has three terms: 3v, 150s, and 50. Each term is its own piece — they are not combined unless they are like terms (you'll learn about that next lesson).
